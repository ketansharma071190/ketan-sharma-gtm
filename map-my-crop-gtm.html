The revenue story for your next raise.

An outside read of Map My Crop’s go-to-market, sharpened after meeting your team. A point of view, not a finished plan. Tell me where I am wrong.

Ketan Sharma  ·  Enterprise GTM, AI into BFSI  ·  Pune  ·  ketansharma071190@gmail.com
01
The moment you are in

You are at $1M ARR and building toward a raise. The question is not the product. It is whether the revenue story is fundable.

The technology is clearly real. What an investor underwrites next is not another model metric. It is a commercial function that turns revenue into a number, without the founders in every deal.

02
The honest read

You built the first $1M the hard way, one farmer at a time.

  • Roughly a few thousand rupees a year, across tens of thousands of farmers. As I understood it from our conversation.
  • It is real revenue and it proves the product works in the field.
  • But it is low ACV, high touch, and high effort per rupee.
  • That is not a criticism. It is the proof that earns you the right to the next motion.
03
The ceiling

That model gets you to $2M with twice the work. It does not get you a Series A narrative.

  • Farmer-by-farmer revenue is linear: more revenue means proportionally more effort. That is effort scaling, not a function scaling.
  • Selling to everyone, everywhere, across geographies dilutes the story rather than sharpening it.
  • Investors fund leverage, not a treadmill. They pay for a function that compounds.
  • The next $1M should be easier to win than the first, not harder.
04
The unlock, already inside your product

The institution that needs the farmer’s data pays many times what the farmer pays, and it is the stickiest revenue you can build.

Your own site already names Insurance and Banking as a segment, and your Command Center already ships a Risk Intelligence Suite. The wedge is built. It is just not being sold as the lead motion. This is the revenue this function is built to win.

05
Why the timing is now

India just made satellite yield data a compliance line item, not a nice-to-have.

  • The PMFBY and YES-TECH scheme pushes crop insurers toward satellite yield estimation. As I understand it, you know this better than I do.
  • That turns your data from a sale you have to make into a budget a buyer already has.
  • SatSure built a company on this lane in India, with HDFC, ICICI and Kotak as clients and investors.
  • Read that as the lane is bankable and funds itself, not as you are behind.
06
Why me, specifically

I have already built this exact motion, into the exact buyer, across markets.

  • 0 to $1M ARR in 12 months at Simplifai, then grown to $1.7M, selling AI into banks and insurers.
  • Named, skeptical, regulated buyers: Kotak, Bajaj, HDFC Mutual Fund, Upstox.
  • Stood up an enterprise org across APAC from zero: the multi-market muscle for your 80-plus-country footprint.
  • Honest split: those are buyers I sold into. The insurers in the plan are where I would start, not relationships I am claiming.
07
The gap is the asset

I do not speak GIS. Neither does the underwriting head I would sell to.

  • I will not pretend three weeks makes me a remote-sensing expert.
  • The bottleneck in these deals is not soil science. It is how a risk committee buys, how to survive a long tender, and how to turn a number into something an auditor signs.
  • My job is to translate your science into their loss-ratio language.
  • I lean on your team for the science. I own the room where the budget sits.
08
The play, India first then global

One sequenced, named-account motion beats selling to everyone everywhere.

  • Wave 1, India beachhead: ICICI Lombard, HDFC Ergo, Bajaj Allianz, Agriculture Insurance Company of India.
  • Wave 2: agri-lending and KCC desks at HDFC, ICICI, Kotak, and agri-NBFCs.
  • Wave 3: reinsurers GIC Re, Swiss Re, Munich Re, where loss-ratio pain concentrates.
  • Then template the same motion across your global footprint. India leads because the mandate is here.
  • These are targets I would pursue, not relationships I am claiming.
09
The function, so you can forecast it

The commercial function, run as a machine you forecast against, not in the founders’ heads.

  • Stages: ICP, Qualify, Co-create the pilot, Land, Handoff to delivery.
  • Qualify bar: a live compliance mandate or an owned loss problem. If neither, disqualify fast.
  • It lives in a CRM, with one number reviewed every week.
  • A handoff contract to delivery and a post-sale signal, so a landed account does not churn.
10
Aligned to the raise

By the time you raise, the deck has a different slide.

Days 0–30
Live the product, lock the institutional ICP, build a 30-account hit-list, write the outbound and the pilot offer.
Days 31–60
Outbound into the wedge, real discovery with insurers and lenders, 2 to 3 pilots scoped, the deck rebuilt around the risk buyer.
By the raise
The commercial function stood up and producing a number, with marquee BFSI logos in pilot. That is the slide that lifts the round.

Modest and held-to, not a hockey stick. Enterprise and government cycles are long and compliance-gated, and I respect that.

11
The role

Build and own the commercial function.

  • You have proven the revenue exists. What is missing is the commercial function that turns it into a number your raise can underwrite. That is the job I want to build and own.
  • Owning the function means owning the number and the motion behind it: who we target, how we qualify, what we price, how it closes, how it compounds. Where marketing and customer success plug in, I build the plan and bring the right hands.
  • The institutional revenue already in your Risk Intelligence Suite is where this gets fundable. I would own opening it, pricing it, and making the first wins repeatable.
  • Head-of-revenue altitude, close to the raise, so the GTM story in the room matches the one on the ground.
  • If there is a fit on both sides, the next conversation is how we close this, not another deck.
