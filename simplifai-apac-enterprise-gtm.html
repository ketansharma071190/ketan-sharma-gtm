Featured case study

How we built APAC enterprise GTM for a Norway-based AI SaaS company.

The work was not just selling AI automation. It was entering India with no local logos, selling into BFSI before the trust stack was obvious, creating a POC wedge through the right technical buyer, and turning one enterprise foothold into a repeatable APAC motion.

$0-$1M ARR in 12 months
$210K enterprise TCV
$70K enterprise ACV
15 reps built across APAC

Starting point

No local logos. No easy trust transfer. Enterprise buyers who had every reason to wait.

The company had credible Scandinavian customers, but no India logos to reduce perceived risk.

The target segment included BFSI buyers, where security, compliance, and internal technical validation mattered before commercial conviction.

At the time, the enterprise trust stack was still being built, so security, compliance, and implementation confidence had to be handled through the sales motion itself.

The product category was AI automation, which created interest quickly but required a careful route from curiosity to department-level adoption.

The move

Find the buyer who could test the risk before the business buyer had to carry it.

01

Mapped the trust problem

The blocker was not interest in AI. It was enterprise risk: data, compliance, implementation confidence, and whether a foreign AI vendor could be trusted in India.

02

Found the AI evaluation wedge

Instead of forcing a top-down commercial pitch too early, we identified the internal AI/innovation team and used a narrow POC to earn technical credibility.

03

Built CTO-level confidence

The motion moved through senior technical validation, not generic sales enthusiasm. The buyer needed to believe the system could work safely before the business case expanded.

04

Converted proof into entry

The POC became the bridge into the real department: a smaller technical win created the permission to discuss operational impact and commercial expansion.

05

Used the first BFSI win as market proof

Once the first credible enterprise foothold existed, the conversation with adjacent BFSI and financial-services buyers changed. The market no longer had to imagine trust from zero.

06

Coached the motion into the team

The play was not founder magic. It became a repeatable motion: target the right technical wedge, prove safely, map stakeholders, then expand into commercial value.

What this proves

Enterprise GTM is not just pipeline. It is trust sequencing.

Constraint

No local reference base

Scandinavian logos helped, but India enterprise buyers needed local confidence before internal risk owners would support the decision.

Route

Technical POC before full commercial push

The AI/innovation team created a safer first step, which opened the door to CTO-level confidence and department-level adoption.

Expansion

One credible BFSI foothold changed the market conversation

The next financial-services conversations had stronger proof, stronger risk handling, and better enterprise pattern recognition.

System

From isolated win to APAC sales motion

The pattern became a teachable sales motion: trust wedge, technical validation, stakeholder map, business case, procurement path, expansion logic.

Operator lesson

The founder cannot be the only person who knows how to sell through risk.

Enterprise sales teams fail when the hard parts stay implicit: why this buyer trusts us, which stakeholder can validate risk, what proof matters, when to push commercial value, and which objection is real versus procedural.

Qualification changed

Not just company size or industry. We qualified for internal technical ownership, risk tolerance, proof path, and operational pain strong enough to survive procurement.

Messaging changed

The pitch moved from AI excitement to operational safety, measurable automation value, and a low-risk way to test before scale.

Hiring and coaching changed

Reps needed to sell through enterprise risk, not just run demos. Coaching focused on stakeholder control, qualification, and next-step discipline.

Pipeline inspection changed

A deal was not real because someone liked the product. It was real when the trust path, technical owner, business pain, and next decision were visible.

Apply this to your motion

If your enterprise deals still need founder context to survive, the system is not built yet.
