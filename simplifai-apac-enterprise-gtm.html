CASE STUDY · Simplifai, AI into Indian BFSI

Selling AI into banks that did not trust AI yet.

Simplifai was a Norway AI company with credible Scandinavian logos and zero presence in India. The job was not to demo automation. It was to enter a market with no local proof, sell into BFSI before the trust stack was obvious, and turn one hard-won foothold into a repeatable APAC motion. The first $1M of ARR came within twelve months of launching GTM, and it grew to $1.7M, carrying a larger annual target into the next year.

The starting point

No local logos. No easy trust transfer. Buyers with every reason to wait.

Credible Scandinavian customers, but not a single India logo to lower perceived risk.

The segment was BFSI, where security, compliance and internal technical validation come before any commercial conviction.

The enterprise trust stack was still being built, so security and implementation confidence had to be carried by the sales motion itself.

AI automation created fast interest and slow adoption. Curiosity was easy. Department-level commitment was not.

The motion, step by step

Enterprise GTM here was not pipeline. It was trust sequencing.

The operator lesson

The founder cannot be the only person who knows how to sell through risk.

Enterprise teams stall when the hard parts stay implicit: why this buyer trusts us, who validates risk, what proof matters, when to push commercial value, and which objection is real versus procedural. The whole point was to make that judgment teachable.

Qualification changed

Not company size or industry. We qualified for internal technical ownership, risk tolerance, a proof path, and pain strong enough to survive procurement.

Messaging changed

From AI excitement to operational safety, measurable automation value, and a low-risk way to test before scale.

Hiring and coaching changed

Reps had to sell through enterprise risk, not just run demos. Coaching focused on stakeholder control, qualification, and next-step discipline.

Pipeline inspection changed

A deal was not real because someone liked the product. It was real when the trust path, technical owner, business pain, and next decision were all visible.

"The blocker was never interest in AI. It was risk. Once one bank had safely said yes, every conversation after it started from a different place."

Why it travels

This is the same motion I now install for founders selling AI into BFSI and complex enterprise: find the buyer who can test the risk before the business buyer has to carry it, prove safely, then turn the first win into a system the team can run without the founder in every room.
